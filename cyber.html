Overview

As AI and vibe coding come to the forefront of programming culture, cyber threats have become increasingly common and much easier to execute. College students are a particularly attractive target — they use shared networks, handle valuable research data, and often lack formal security training.

Malware

Malware (short for malicious software) is code written specifically to harm users or systems — destroying data, stealing information, or rendering infected systems unusable.

💰

Ransomware

Locks your data or device and threatens to keep it inaccessible — or release sensitive information — unless a ransom is paid. Universities are prime targets because of the value of research data.

🐴

Trojan Horse

Malicious code hidden within seemingly useful programs. It tricks users into downloading it, then opens backdoors or installs additional malware once it has a foothold in the system.

⚠ Real Case: Penn State 2015 Two cyberattacks — one originating from China — targeted Penn State's College of Engineering using advanced malware. The university was forced to disconnect from the internet entirely. Student usernames and passwords were compromised, leading to mandatory two-factor authentication.

Phishing & Social Engineering

Social engineering — sometimes called "human hacking" — manipulates users into exposing confidential information. It exploits trust, urgency, and familiarity rather than technical vulnerabilities.

🎣

Phishing

Attackers use rogue links, fake emails, and spoofed phone calls to trick people into sharing personal information or sending money. Spear phishing is a targeted version aimed specifically at you.

🎯 At UVA Right Now Many students are currently receiving phishing emails in their Outlook junk folders — fake high-paying remote job offers and impersonated Microsoft emails claiming passwords have expired. Always verify before clicking any link.

Password Attacks

Attackers obtain user passwords either through phishing or by using brute-force — trying as many combinations as possible until one is correct. Weak or reused passwords make this significantly easier.

🔑

Credential Stuffing

Attackers take username/password pairs from one data breach and try them across hundreds of other sites. If you reuse passwords, one breach exposes all your accounts.

Network & Lab Security

Research labs present unique cybersecurity risks. IoT devices — sensors, instruments, connected equipment — often run outdated firmware and weak authentication, creating direct entry points for attackers seeking research data.

📡 Lab Environment Risk IoT malware attacks on universities rose 961% since 2022. Unsecured lab devices with default passwords create direct pathways to sensitive research networks. Always follow your lab's network security protocols.

UVA-Specific Threats

The University of Michigan experienced a two-day internet shutdown at the start of the 2023 school year due to a significant cybersecurity incident. Students had to rely on cell phones and off-campus networks to attend lectures. Similar disruptions could happen at UVA.

Ongoing phishing campaigns at UVA target thousands of students and faculty regularly. Even security-aware users can be tricked. Attackers are sophisticated and constantly refining their tactics.

Best Practices Checklist

  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all university accounts
  • Use a unique, complex password for every account (consider a password manager)
  • Verify the sender before clicking any link or attachment in email
  • Keep your operating system and software up to date
  • Avoid using public Wi-Fi without a VPN
  • Never plug in unknown USB drives
  • Report suspicious emails to UVA IT Security immediately
  • Back up research data to university-approved storage regularly
